Four Return Models

1. Normal Distribution (Baseline)

The standard assumption in most retirement calculators. Returns follow a multivariate normal distribution with parameters based on historical averages.

2. Fat-Tailed Distribution (Student's t)

Uses a Student's t-distribution with 5 degrees of freedom to capture more frequent extreme events. This better reflects historical market crashes and booms that are "impossible" under normal assumptions.

3. Mean Reversion (AR(1) Process)

Models returns with negative autocorrelation (φ = -0.3), meaning poor returns tend to be followed by better-than-average returns. This reflects the hypothesis that markets revert to their long-term mean.

4. Historical Bootstrap

Randomly samples actual historical returns (1926-2023), preserving all empirical properties including correlation structure, fat tails, and any serial correlation present in the data.
